## 1. Meta policy state (2025-2026)

### 1.1 AI-generated content disclosure

Mandatory advertiser self-disclosure of digitally created or altered media applies only to ads about social issues, elections, or politics. Disclosure is required when such an ad contains a photorealistic image or video, or realistic-sounding audio, digitally created or altered to depict a real person saying or doing something they did not, to depict a realistic-looking person that does not exist or a realistic-looking event that did not happen, or to present a fabricated recording of a real event. Inconsequential edits (crops, color correction, image size) are exempt. Non-disclosure leads to ad rejection; repeated failure brings advertiser penalties. Disclosures surface on the ad and in the Ad Library. Sources: Meta announcement coverage, TechCrunch, 2023-11-08, https://techcrunch.com/2023/11/08/meta-will-start-requiring-disclosures-for-political-ads-manipulated-with-ai/ (accessed 2026-06-10); Social Media Today on the rules taking effect, https://www.socialmediatoday.com/news/metas-new-disclosure-requirements-digitally-altered-ad-content/704266/ (accessed 2026-06-10).

For ordinary commercial ads there is no Meta rule that mandates a "this is AI" disclosure as such, but Meta applies automatic "AI info" labels through three detection paths: embedded provenance metadata (IPTC Digital Source Type, C2PA manifests), proprietary classifiers, and advertiser self-disclosure in Ads Manager. Primary confirmation: Meta's newsroom post "Expanding GenAI Transparency for Meta's Ads Products" (published 2025-02-03, updated 2026-06-01, https://about.fb.com/news/2025/02/gen-ai-transparency-metas-ads-products/, fetched 2026-06-10) states that ads made with Meta's own generative AI tools get an "AI info" label when edits are significant or a photorealistic human is generated, that ads containing AI-generated photorealistic humans carry the label next to the "Sponsored" tag rather than behind the three-dot menu, and that Meta will "automatically detect ads created or edited using third-party AI tools through industry-standard signals" and label them inside the new "About this ad" surface, rolling out through 2025-2026. [UNCERTAIN, secondary source] A May 2026 industry analysis states that by 2026 this functions as an enforced advertising requirement rather than optional disclosure, that undisclosed-but-detected photorealistic AI in ads is treated as a disclosure failure, and that penalties include rejection, retroactive flagging, and distribution suppression of up to roughly 80 percent for deceptive AI video with synthetic audio. These enforcement specifics are not confirmed by a Meta primary document in this session and should be treated as directional. Source: AuditSocials, "Meta AI Content Label Policy 2026", 2026-05-18, https://www.auditsocials.com/blog/meta-ai-generated-content-label-policy-2026 (accessed 2026-06-10).

Practical reading for Mira: a photorealistic avatar that "does not exist" is exactly the media class Meta's labeling machinery targets. Stripping provenance metadata to dodge the label converts a benign label into a deception finding. The safe posture is to keep provenance intact and self-disclose where the flow offers it.

### 1.2 Health, beauty, and cosmetic-procedure ad rules

Meta's Health and Wellness ad standard (the successor that absorbed the old Personal Health and Appearance policy) imposes, per the Transparency Center page fetched 2026-06-10 (https://transparency.meta.com/policies/ad-standards/restricted-goods-services/health-wellness/):

- Ads for weight loss and for cosmetic procedures and surgeries must be targeted 18+.
- No side-by-side before-and-after comparisons for weight-loss products or for skin anti-aging treatments such as Botox and dermal fillers. No "close up on specific body area by pinching fat" style imagery.
- General cosmetic procedures (the page's examples include breast augmentation, rhinoplasty) may show before-and-after transformations "without employing negative self-perception tactics".
- Skin anti-aging ads may use zoomed-in or close-up images but they "must reflect realistic outcomes over time and should not include side-by-side comparisons".
- Ads must not "imply or attempt to generate negative self-perception or declare there is a perfect body type or appearance one should aspire to".

Note the asymmetry: the anti-aging and injectables segment, which is the medspa core, is the segment where before-and-after side-by-sides are banned on Meta even though some surgical cosmetic categories may show transformations.

Personal attributes: Meta ads must not assert or imply that the advertiser knows personal attributes of the viewer, including physical or mental health or medical condition. Second-person framing such as "Are you struggling with acne?" is the classic rejection trigger for beauty ads. [UNCERTAIN on exact current policy text: the Transparency Center personal-attributes URL returned 404 during this session, suggesting the page moved; the rule itself is well established.] Source: AdsUploader Meta ad guidelines compendium, https://adsuploader.com/blog/meta-ad-guidelines (accessed 2026-06-10).

Unrealistic outcomes and unapproved health claims are rejection grounds independent of imagery. Source: AdAmigo analysis, https://www.adamigo.ai/blog/meta-ads-policy-unapproved-health-claims-explained (accessed 2026-06-10).

### 1.3 January 2025 health-and-wellness data restrictions

Starting January 2025 Meta restricts advertisers whose web or app data sources are categorized as health and wellness from using lower-funnel conversion events (Purchase, Add to Cart, checkout, booked appointments) for optimization via Pixel and Conversions API; affected advertisers must optimize on upper-funnel events such as landing page views or engagement. Categorization is automatic and triggered by condition or treatment content on the destination. A second wave extending restrictions toward healthcare lead generation was anticipated for early 2026. Sources: Foley Hoag, "Meta's New Advertising Rules: Key Considerations for Health and Wellness Businesses", January 2025, https://foleyhoag.com/news-and-insights/blogs/security-privacy-and-the-law/2025/january/meta-s-new-advertising-rules-key-considerations-for-health-and-wellness-businesses/ (accessed 2026-06-10); EHM Results 2025-2026 overview, https://ehmresults.com/meta-ad-2025-2026-restrictions-what-healthcare-practices-need-to-know/ (accessed 2026-06-10); Digital Position, https://www.digitalposition.com/resources/blog/ppc/metas-new-restrictions-on-health-and-wellness-ads-what-you-need-to-know/ (accessed 2026-06-10). ### 1.4 AI likenesses of real people in ads

Meta actively litigates and engineers against synthetic likenesses of real people endorsing products without consent ("celeb-bait"). Meta has sued deceptive advertising operations using AI-generated celebrity likenesses, and runs facial-recognition matching that compares faces in ads against public figures' profile photos to detect impersonation. A class action over deepfake doctor endorsements is also in play. ## 2. Click-to-WhatsApp (CTWA) constraints

### 2.1 Setup prerequisites that block or misroute ads

- An active WhatsApp Business account is required and the WhatsApp number must be verified and connected to the Facebook Page and Business Manager. Unverified or incorrectly linked numbers prevent publication; wrong-number assignment silently routes ad conversations to the wrong inbox. Sources: Woztell CTWA guides, https://support.woztell.com/portal/en/kb/articles/how-to-create-click-to-whatsapp-with-meta-ads and https://support.woztell.com/portal/en/kb/articles/guide-to-click-to-whatsapp-with-meta-ads (accessed 2026-06-10); Meta Business Help, "Create Ads that Click to WhatsApp in Ads Manager", https://www.facebook.com/business/help/447934475640650 (login-walled; accessed 2026-06-10).
- Supported objectives: Engagement (WhatsApp conversation location) and Leads. Traffic is technically possible but optimizes for clicks rather than conversations and is advised against. CTA should be "Send Message" or "Chat on WhatsApp"; mismatched CTAs like "Learn More" hurt review and performance. Formats: single image, video, carousel, collection. Practical creative limits: video under 60 seconds performs best; primary text under roughly 125 characters avoids feed truncation. Source: AdLibrary CTWA guide, 2026-05-17, https://adlibrary.com/posts/meta-click-to-whatsapp-ads-guide (accessed 2026-06-10).
- The pre-filled conversation opener is part of the ad configuration; specific openers with up to 3 quick replies outperform generic ones. Same source.

### 2.2 Policy layer specific to WhatsApp destinations

The WhatsApp Business Messaging Policy and Commerce Policy apply on top of Meta ad standards. Healthcare is permitted: doctors, medical practices, hospitals, and clinics may message and may sell services (visits, procedures), explicitly including "body enhancement and modification clinics", but may not sell items (drugs, medical devices) through WhatsApp commerce surfaces. The medical-services allowance excludes businesses operating in the US and France, which does not affect SG/MY. Advertisers must gate messaging per age, geography, and regulatory requirements of their vertical. Sources: WhatsApp Business Messaging Policy, https://business.whatsapp.com/policy (accessed 2026-06-10); tyntec FAQ on health industries on WhatsApp, https://www.tyntec.com/helpcenter/docs/faqs/whatsapp-business/whatsapp-commerce-policy/what-industries-in-the-health-sector-are-allowed-on-whatsapp/ (accessed 2026-06-10); WuSeller commerce policy guide (2026), https://www.wuseller.com/whatsapp-business-knowledge-hub/whatsapp-business-commerce-policy-full-prohibited-list-compliance-guide-2026/ (accessed 2026-06-10).

### 2.3 Review gotchas

Common CTWA rejection drivers mirror Meta health-ad rules: implied personal attributes (medical condition), restricted goods, misleading or quick-fix health claims, and sensational content. WhatsApp also publishes rejection guidance for ads created from the WhatsApp Business side. Sources: WhatsApp Help Center, "Why was my ad or ad account rejected on the WhatsApp Business App?", https://faq.whatsapp.com/594356815709445 (accessed 2026-06-10); Conv3rt disapproval overview, https://conv3rt.co.uk/articles/what-are-common-reasons-for-meta-ad-disapprovals/ (accessed 2026-06-10).

## 3. Singapore and Malaysia: law and medical-advertising rules

### 3.1 Singapore: healthcare advertising (HCSA)

The Healthcare Services (Advertisement) Regulations 2021 (in force 2022-01-03) govern all advertising by licensed healthcare providers, including aesthetic clinics. Primary text: https://sso.agc.gov.sg/SL/HSA2020-S1033-2021 (403 to non-browser fetch this session). The provisions below are primary-confirmed via MOH's official FAQs on the HCS (Advertisement) Regulations, version 1.1, updated 22 Nov 2024, read in full this session: https://isomer-user-content.by.gov.sg/7/1d84b66a-5625-49a2-8c0e-e06eea4ed9fd/FAQs%20on%20HCS%20(Advertisement)%20Regulations_1.1.pdf (fetched 2026-06-10).

- Who may advertise: only an HCSA licensee or a person acting on the licensee's authority (an "authorised person") may advertise licensable healthcare services (HCSA s.31(1)). Anyone else commits an offence: fine up to SGD 20,000 and/or 12 months imprisonment, plus up to SGD 1,000 per day for continuing offences (FAQ Q3). Authorised persons (agencies, platforms, software operators) are themselves liable under the Regulations for non-compliant ads, appointments should be documented contractually, and the licensee remains ultimately responsible for ads its authorised persons publish (FAQ Q3, Q4, Q54). MOH confirmed in Parliament that influencers and other non-licensees cannot advertise aesthetic treatments without licence or authorisation (2024-01-09, https://www.moh.gov.sg/newsroom/regulation-of-advertisements-for-aesthetic-treatments/, fetched 2026-06-10).
- Substantiation: ad content must be capable of being substantiated with credible evidence (peer-reviewed or locally accepted literature), cited in the ad and producible to MOH on request (FAQ Q14, Reg 5(1)(a)).
- Laudatory statements (subjective praise, commendations, compliments) are "strictly prohibited, regardless of whether they are factually accurate or not" (FAQ Q16; MOH Annex A lists likely-laudatory terms).
- Before-and-after: advertisements "must not feature before-and-after or only after treatment photographs, pictures, videos or films, even with disclaimers"; such material may only be shown to patients during consultation with proper context (FAQ Q15).
- Soliciting or encouraging consumption (Reg 5(1)(g), FAQ Q17) prohibits: before-and-after photos; results-within-a-time-period claims ("straight teeth in 2 weeks", "instant whitening"); free or discounted services; interest-free payment plans; price comparisons; "as low as" or "lowest prices" framing; "package" tied to a discount; stating an instalment amount without the total price. Exact prices may be listed plainly, without prefixes or comparative listings.
- Testimonials and reviews: Regulation 14 governs reviews, testimonials, and endorsements. Paid reviews are prohibited, and posting pictures of or reviews by celebrity or influencer patients is restricted (FAQ Q39-Q46 headings; the detailed answers on what unpaid or own-channel reviews remain permissible were not extracted this session). The earlier secondary claim that a testimonial given directly to the licensee may appear unaltered on the licensee's own website remains [UNCERTAIN]; confirm against FAQ Q40/Q44 and the regulation text before relying on it.
- Territorial scope: the rules apply to any ad with a Singapore link, meaning a person physically in Singapore can access it or it is addressed to people known to be in Singapore, including the licensee's own website even when targeting foreigners (FAQ Q9).
- No pre-vetting: MOH does not review or vet specific advertisements for compliance; the burden sits with the licensee and its authorised persons, and enforcement is complaint-driven and active (FAQ Q8; MOH newsroom above).

Corroborating secondary sources: The Health Collective SG, "HCSA Advertising Regulations", 2025-02-21, https://www.thehealthcollective.sg/articles/phmc-hcsa-advertising-regulations (fetched 2026-06-10); First Page Digital summary, https://www.firstpagedigital.sg/resources/business/singapores-medical-marketing-regulations-guidelines/ (accessed 2026-06-10).

Aesthetic procedures additionally sit under SMC's Guidelines on Aesthetic Practices and the Aesthetic Procedure Oversight Committee (List A/B certification of practitioners). Source: SMC, https://www.smc.gov.sg/for-professionals/regulations-guidelines-circulars/guidelines-on-aesthetic-practices-for-doctors/ (accessed 2026-06-10). [UNCERTAIN] Botulinum toxin and fillers are HSA-registered health products and botulinum toxin is prescription-only; advertising prescription-only medicines to the public is prohibited in Singapore, which is why clinics advertise generic procedure language rather than drug brand names. Verify exact instrument (Health Products Act advertising regulations) before relying on it; HSA aesthetic-related guideline: https://www.hsa.gov.sg/docs/default-source/hprg-mdb/aesthetic-related-guidelines_2018.pdf (accessed 2026-06-10).

### 3.2 Singapore: synthetic media, deepfakes, ASAS, PDPA

There is no general statute requiring AI labels on commercial advertising in Singapore as of this research date. The synthetic-media instruments are targeted: the Elections (Integrity of Online Advertising) (Amendment) Act 2024 bans misrepresentative digitally generated content of candidates during election periods (GovTech deployed deepfake detection for GE2025); the Online Safety (Relief and Accountability) Act 2025, passed 2025-11-05, creates an Online Safety Commission and statutory torts commencing 2026-06-29, covering 13 categories of online harms including online impersonation and inauthentic material, meaning deepfake or digitally altered realistic content presented as real (primary: MLAW, https://www.mlaw.gov.sg/online-safety-commission-and-online-safety-relief-and-accountability-act-2025-to-start-on-29-june-2026/, accessed 2026-06-10; analysis: https://www.covenantchambers.com/articles/singapore-introduces-a-landmark-new-online-safety-relief-and-accountability-law, accessed 2026-06-10); the Criminal Law (Miscellaneous Amendments) Bill 2025 extends intimate-image offences to wholly AI-generated images; POFMA covers falsehoods against public interest. None of these target a fictional commercial spokesperson. Sources: Withers/Conventus Law analysis, https://conventuslaw.com/report/deepfakes-in-singapore-legal-recourse-regulatory-powers-and-building-long-term-corporate-resilience/ (accessed 2026-06-10); GovTech on INDEPTH and GE2025, https://www.tech.gov.sg/technews/deepfakes-and-the-crisis-of-credibility/ (accessed 2026-06-10).

The advertising self-regulator is where the synthetic-spokesperson risk concentrates. ASAS reported 379 pieces of ad feedback in 2025, including seven about generative AI, more than double the 2023-2024 combined total. The on-point precedent: a telemedicine company used Gen AI to create a first-person testimonial video that gave the impression of coming from a real individual; after complaints the advertiser halted the campaign. ASAS's stated position: "False and misleading advertisement content is unacceptable, regardless of the tools used or the disclosures added", and it favors a risk-based approach focusing labeling or restrictions where Gen AI has high potential to mislead, noting that indiscriminate vague AI disclosures hinder consumer discernment. Source: Marketing-Interactive, 2026-03-23, https://www.marketing-interactive.com/misleading-ai-ad-complaints-on-the-rise-says-asas (fetched 2026-06-10). The SCAP baseline is that ads be legal, decent, honest, truthful: https://asas.org.sg/About/Code (accessed 2026-06-10).

PDPA: identifiable images and voice recordings are personal data (an individual "may be identified from his voice" where the clip is sufficiently clear), so collecting and using a real person's face and voice to build an avatar requires consent and notification under the PDPA. The PDPC's March 2024 Advisory Guidelines on the Use of Personal Data in AI Recommendation and Decision Systems explicitly do not cover generative AI; PDPC signaled future guidance on personal data in generative-AI training. A fully synthetic persona that does not identify any real individual is not personal data. Sources: PDPC advisory guidelines, https://www.pdpc.gov.sg/guidelines-and-consultation/2024/02/advisory-guidelines-on-use-of-personal-data-in-ai-recommendation-and-decision-systems (accessed 2026-06-10); PDPC selected-topics guidelines (May 2024 revision), https://www.pdpc.gov.sg/-/media/files/pdpc/pdf-files/advisory-guidelines/ag-on-selected-topics/advisory-guidelines-on-the-pdpa-for-selected-topics-(revised-may-2024).pdf (accessed 2026-06-10); Norton Rose Fulbright summary, https://www.dataprotectionreport.com/2024/03/singapore-releases-new-guidelines-on-the-use-of-personal-data-in-ai-systems/ (accessed 2026-06-10). ### 3.3 Malaysia: medical and aesthetic advertising

- Medicine Advertisements Board (MAB) approval, evidenced by a KKLIU number displayed on every advertisement, is required before publishing any advertisement to the public that makes medical or treatment claims, including services for skin and beauty conditions; this is grounded in the Medicines (Advertisement and Sale) Act 1956 and the Medicine Advertisements Board Regulations 1976. Approval issuance is about 5 working days for compliant applications. Pure cosmetic advertising with no medical claims falls under the Cosmetic Advertising Code and does not need MAB approval. Sources: MyHealth MOH portal, http://www.myhealth.gov.my/en/the-role-of-medicine-advertisements-board/ (timeout on fetch; cited from search index, accessed 2026-06-10); Pharmaceutical Services Programme, https://pharmacy.moh.gov.my/en/content/how-recognize-approved-advertisement.html (accessed 2026-06-10); BioPrestige overview (confirms KKLIU display duty and 1976 Regulations), https://bioprestige.my/medicine-advertisements-board-mab-regulating-medical-advertising-malaysia/ (fetched 2026-06-10); Disruptive Doctors guide, https://disruptive-doctors.com/kkliu-advertising-guidelines-malaysia/ (403 on fetch; accessed via search 2026-06-10).
- The MAB Advertising Guidelines for Healthcare Facilities and Services (private hospitals, clinics, radiological clinics, medical laboratories), Amendment MAB 3/2023, in effect 2023-07-05, were read in full this session (primary): https://pharmacy.moh.gov.my/sites/default/files/document-upload/advertising-guidelines-healthcare-facilities-and-services-mab-3.2023.pdf (fetched 2026-06-10). Key rules: advertisements may be publicised only upon MAB approval unless exempt (s.1.3); content must be factually accurate, capable of substantiation, and not exaggerated, false, misleading, or deceptive (s.2.2); promotion of an individual practitioner's skills, knowledge, and experience is not allowed (s.4.2); patient testimonials are permitted only about premises (cleanliness, staff friendliness, environment), and testimonials about treatment, services, or practitioner expertise are NOT allowed (s.4.5); celebrity endorsements, with "celebrity" defined to include influencers, models, athletes, professionals, and patients receiving treatment, are likewise premises-only and NOT allowed for treatment or practitioner skill (s.4.6); direct or implied comparisons between healthcare facilities are prohibited (s.5.1); website content must be informative and simple "without laudatory remarks" (s.6.8); packages, discounts, and price reductions ARE allowed in service ads (s.7.1.1), but price comparisons between providers (s.7.1.2), free offers of treatment (s.7.1.3), and gifts (s.7.2) are not; ads placed outside Malaysia that are accessible to the Malaysian public online must also conform (s.9.1). Note the divergence from Singapore: Malaysia permits plain discounts and packages while Singapore bans them; both ban treatment testimonials.

## 4. Provider terms (HeyGen, Kling, ElevenLabs)

### 4.1 HeyGen (avatar video)

The depicted person (the Actor) goes through HeyGen's consent verification (identity confirmation plus authorization recording). Actors may request removal at any time, and users are responsible for honoring removal requests "wherever the content has been published". This creates a contractual takedown obligation that reaches already-published ads. Source: HeyGen moderation policy, https://www.heygen.com/moderation-policy (fetched 2026-06-10); HeyGen terms, https://www.heygen.com/terms (accessed 2026-06-10).
- Commercial use: paid plans license generated video for commercial purposes including paid advertising campaigns. Source: HeyGen FAQ coverage, https://flowith.io/blog/heygen-faq-custom-avatar-api-limits-commercial-rights/ (accessed 2026-06-10). [UNCERTAIN on plan-tier specifics; confirm current plan terms.]
- Content rules: deceptive content is prohibited; health information and false-claims content sits in a "conditional" bucket that HeyGen may restrict at its discretion. The moderation policy has no explicit healthcare-advertising carve-out, so medspa scripts ride on the deception and illegality clauses. Source: https://www.heygen.com/moderation-policy (fetched 2026-06-10).

### 4.2 ElevenLabs (synthetic voice)

From the Prohibited Use Policy, https://elevenlabs.io/use-policy (fetched 2026-06-10):

- Consent: prohibited to create audio replicating another person's voice "without consent or legal right". Form of consent is not specified by the policy itself, but separate state-law analyses recommend written consent specifying use case, duration, and platforms. Secondary: Terms.Law, https://terms.law/ai-output-rights/elevenlabs/ (accessed 2026-06-10).
- Deception: prohibited to use audio "in a manner intended to deceive others about whether the voice was generated by artificial intelligence". This is effectively a disclosure-adjacent obligation for synthetic spokespeople.
- Commercial use: free tier prohibits "any commercial purpose, including for advertising"; commercial rights come with paid plans.
- Health content: prohibited to provide "Health or medical advice, including diagnosis, treatment, and mental health services" without "a qualified professional in that field reviewing the Output before it is made available". Treatment-describing ad scripts for clinics sit near this line; a practitioner review step satisfies it.
- Conversational AI products must "clearly and prominently disclose to their users they are interacting with AI rather than a human", including approved patient-facing healthcare uses. This targets interactive agents more than ad voiceovers, but signals the provider's posture.

## 5. Implications for Mira's consent/identity layer and publish gate (ranked)

1. Block first-person patient-voice creative for healthcare orgs in both SG and MY; this is now primary-confirmed on both sides. The flagship UGC format, a synthetic person saying "I got this treatment and here is my result", is simultaneously (a) a testimonial, banned in SG healthcare advertising under the HCSA Advertisement Regulations (Reg 14; laudatory content separately banned by MOH FAQ Q16 regardless of factual accuracy), (b) a treatment testimonial and influencer-style endorsement, banned in MY under MAB 3/2023 ss.4.5-4.6 where premises-only praise (cleanliness, staff friendliness, environment) is the lone carve-out, and (c) a fabricated testimonial, which ASAS has already treated as misleading in the 2026 telemedicine Gen-AI case. The publish gate needs a script classifier that flags testimonial-form copy (first-person outcome claims) and hard-blocks it for orgs in the healthcare vertical, steering Mira toward clinic-voice, educational, or branded-presenter formats. This is the single largest constraint on the current product concept.

2. Hard-block before-and-after and outcome imagery at the gate, twice over. SG HCSAR prohibits before-and-after and even only-after treatment imagery in ads, with disclaimers explicitly unavailing (MOH FAQ Q15), plus time-bound outcome claims, free or discounted services, interest-free plans, price comparisons, and "as low as" framing (FAQ Q17); Meta separately bans side-by-side before-and-after for the anti-aging and injectables segment that is the medspa core. For MY, plain discounts and packages are permitted, but lint out free offers, gifts, and price comparisons (MAB 7.1-7.2). The gate should reject any creative containing comparison composites or result claims for SG clinics regardless of consent records, and apply the Meta anti-aging rule globally. Also enforce 18+ targeting on every cosmetic-procedure campaign at publish time, since this is a Meta requirement Mira can verify mechanically.

3. Make persona class a first-class field in the consent/identity layer with three classes and different obligations. (a) Real-person avatar: requires provider-grade verified consent (HeyGen consent video), plus PDPA consent in SG and explicit consent in MY because face/voice processing is now sensitive biometric data under the amended Malaysian PDPA (in force April 2025); consent records must capture scope (platforms, duration, ad use) and support revocation, and revocation must propagate to live ads because HeyGen obliges users to honor removal wherever content was published. (b) Fully synthetic persona: no data-subject obligations, but must never be presented as a real patient (ties to implication 1) and should carry AI provenance. (c) Prohibited: any avatar resembling an identifiable third party (celebrity, local doctor, influencer); Meta runs face-matching against public figures and litigates synthetic endorsements, so resemblance is an enforcement risk even without intent. The publish gate should refuse to publish when the persona class or its consent artifact is missing.

4. Adopt a keep-provenance, self-disclose posture rather than a label-avoidance posture. No SG or MY statute currently mandates AI labels on commercial ads, Meta's mandatory ad-level disclosure applies only to social-issue/political ads, and ASAS explicitly says vague AI labels do not cure misleadingness. But Meta auto-detects AI via C2PA/IPTC metadata and classifiers, treats detected-undisclosed content unfavorably, and ElevenLabs prohibits deceiving people about AI generation. Malaysia is moving toward mandatory AI labels under the Online Safety Act (in force January 2026) and an AI Bill is expected mid-2026. Meta's 2026-06-01 newsroom update confirms third-party AI detection and "About this ad" labeling are an active rollout, so assume Mira's renders will be machine-labeled regardless of self-disclosure; Kling's terms additionally require visible Kling AI attribution when no brand mark is affixed. Concretely: never strip provenance metadata in the render pipeline, always affix the clinic brand mark, set the Ads Manager AI disclosure where the flow offers it, and store the disclosure state on the WorkTrace so the audit trail shows intent.

5. Add a per-jurisdiction regulatory-approval slot to the publish gate. Malaysian healthcare facility and service ads need MAB approval before publication (primary: MAB 3/2023 s.1.3, unless exempt under s.3.0) and the KKLIU number displayed in the creative; this is an external regulator step that no amount of internal human approval substitutes for. In Singapore the inverse holds: MOH explicitly does not pre-vet ads (FAQ Q8), so the compliance burden lands entirely on Mira's own gate, and Switchboard as an "authorised person" carries direct statutory liability (FAQ Q3) up to SGD 20,000 and/or 12 months imprisonment. For MY orgs the gate should require a KKLIU reference field (or an explicit "cosmetic-claims-only, no approval needed" attestation) before an ad can leave PAUSED; for SG orgs, an HCSA checklist attestation. This fits Switchboard's existing approval-as-lifecycle-state model: model regulator approval as a distinct required approval, not a comment.

6. Plan campaign optimization around Meta's health-and-wellness data restrictions. Medspa landing pages are likely auto-categorized health and wellness, which since January 2025 blocks lower-funnel optimization events (purchases, booked appointments) via Pixel/CAPI, with a second wave expected to reach healthcare lead-gen in 2026. CTWA campaigns optimizing on Conversations Started or engagement are the policy-stable design, which conveniently matches the WhatsApp booking funnel. Riley's optimization loop and any future CAPI attribution must not assume booked-appointment events are available as optimization signals.

7. Keep CTWA plumbing health as a publish precondition. Number-to-Page-to-WABA binding must be verified before publish (unverified or misassigned numbers block ads or misroute patient conversations), objectives restricted to Engagement/Leads with a WhatsApp conversation location, CTA limited to Send Message / Chat on WhatsApp, and the pre-filled opener treated as governed copy (it is part of the reviewed ad). Verify the current free-entry-point pricing window before modeling conversation economics, since WhatsApp moved to per-message pricing in July 2025.

8. Wire provider compliance into the existing human-approval step. ElevenLabs requires a qualified professional to review health-advice output before release: route every treatment-describing script through the clinic practitioner's approval and record that identity on the approval, which satisfies the policy and strengthens the audit story. Enforce paid-tier API keys for all ad audio (free tier prohibits advertising use). For Kling, prohibit uploading clinic-supplied real-person footage unless the consent record covers third-party processing and model-training exposure, since Kling's license over uploads is broad and indemnity is absent.

9. Strip prescription drug brand names from creative copy for SG. Botulinum toxin is prescription-only and advertising prescription medicines to the public is prohibited in Singapore, which is why compliant clinics use generic procedure language. Add brand-name lexicon checks (Botox, Dysport, brand filler names) to the SG creative lint.
